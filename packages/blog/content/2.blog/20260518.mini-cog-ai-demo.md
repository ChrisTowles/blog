## What is the Mini-Cog?

The Mini-Cog is a short cognitive screen used in primary care. It has two parts: a **three-word recall** task and a **clock drawing test**. It takes about three minutes, scores 0–5, and a low score is a prompt for a fuller evaluation — not a diagnosis on its own. It was developed by Soo Borson and James Scanlan and validated in the early 2000s (Borson et al., *J Am Geriatr Soc*, 2003).

## Why have an AI administer it?

The interesting part isn't the test — it's the administration. A screen like this normally needs a person to read the words aloud, watch the clock get drawn, and apply a scoring rubric consistently. That's three things AI is now plausibly good at:

- **Consistency** — the same instructions, the same rubric, every time.
- **Accessibility** — text-to-speech and speech-to-text mean it works without a clinician in the room.
- **Structured scoring** — the clock drawing is graded by a vision model against the six Shulman criteria; the recall is fuzzy-matched (so "chairs" still counts for "chair").

So I built it. Claude Haiku scores the word recall; Claude Opus looks at your clock drawing and grades it.

## Where this stops

This is a toy. It is deliberately *not* a clinical tool, and the gap between the two is the whole point of the post:

- **No data is stored.** Audio and the clock image are sent to the model to score one attempt, then discarded. No database, no analytics, no account.
- **No diagnostic language.** The result screen will never tell you that you "have" or "don't have" anything. It reports a number and tells you to talk to a doctor.
- **The page is `noindex`.** It's a demonstration of a proprietary instrument, not a service.

A clinical-grade implementation would need validation studies, regulatory review, clinician oversight, accessibility audits, and a privacy posture far beyond "we don't store it." Knowing where the demo ends and the responsibility begins is the actual lesson.
